About Tiahuanaco Empire

The Tiwanaku (Tiahuanaco) Empire ruled from around 400 A.D. to 1200 A.D. before the earliest advanced civilizations in the New World, such as the Incas and Aztecs. The Empire expanded through a high-altitude mountain range called "El Altiplano" and controlled territory around Lake Titicaca.

Throughout this whole area, unification and farming were just as important as religion. Early Tiahuanacos believed in multiple gods that controlled their environment and climate, including the Sun, Moon, and Rain Gods. With this heavy emphasis on astrology, they also had believed in animal deities that lived among them. The Puma and Snake are among the most important, symbolizing punishment and danger for those who did not respect the supreme leaders.

The Altiplano is full of harsh living conditions at such a height, making it difficult to breathe, work, and conserve stamina. The climate was even rougher, with high temperatures during the day and frosts during the night. Droughts are not common either. However, the Tiwanaku Empire adapted to their surroundings quickly to provide a habitable space for 1 million people. Their discovery of Coca leaves gave them more stamina to control their airflow and work speed. Innovative farming techniques around Lake Titicaca and their artificial bed planting allowed them to get consistent yields of crops. Early weaving also allowed the Tiahuanaco Empire to use wool from Alpacas and llamas to protect the cold.

The people of the Tiahuanaco Empire concentrated several of their temples and pyramids in the current day town of Tiwanaku, where several tourists come to visit. The construction of these ancient ruins is what surprises several scientists today. Their significances and origins are not widely known and are currently being studied. See "Attractions" to learn more.

Several reasons led to the downfall of the Tiahuanaco Empire. Drought and terrible weather prevented a plentiful harvest and could no longer support such a large population. Politically, the imperial state started to lose its power as a new leader could not be selected and accepted by all. The environment of the Altiplano was truly unsuitable for a nation to thrive. Artifacts and pottery still being found today are being tested to see the exact time when the empire declined.

Dios de los Baculos

The primary deity of the Tiwanaku culture is the "Dios de Los Baculos" (God of Staves). History describes this god as a blessing from Lake Titicaca, which rose from the water to create light. Several civilizations after the Tiahuancos have also worshiped this god, including the Incas, revealing its influence in this region. While not heavily believed in today because of Spanish conquistadors forcing Christianity upon the New World, the Dios de Los Baculos plays a part in defining authentic Tihuanacota culture. As a god that created everything in the unreachable sky, its relevance is great, so great that it is the centerpiece of "La Puerta del Sol." This is why so many souvenirs and textiles are based on the Dios de Los Baculos, as it has become a symbol and representation of the town itself.

Celebracion del Ano Nuevo Andino (Willkakuti)

June 21st marks the winter solstice in Bolivia. This event is vital in the Tiahuanaco and Aymara cultures as it relates to the gods of nature that help the agricultural cycle. The winter begins in South America, so the offerings given are meant to provide safety through the freezing temperatures for the people and farmland. Offerings include llama bait, incense, sugar, coca leaves, etc. Even with all of this, the Tiwanaku descendants offer the "sullu", a cremated llama fetus. Aymaras, Bolivians, and tourists from the western world all come to watch the ritual. Evo Morales, previous president of Bolivia, made Willkauti a national holiday as he is part Aymara from the Tiahuanaco people.

Ceremonial Tiwanaku Pottery

Ceramics were commonly made during the Tiahuanaco Empire. These were representations of gods, deities, and the human condition. Common figures include pumas, snakes, skulls, and gods. These well-painted and structured relics were part of the ritual and ceremony use for sacrifices and prayer. Tiahuanaco pottery defined the religious and spiritual life of the empire, which gives historians evidence of how they lived. Modern-day Tiwanaku adapted this style in their town through building design, souvenirs, and formal china. Currently, ceramics and artifacts are being found today throughout the ruins and Lake Titicaca. So far, archaeologists have found 2,500 ceramic vessels related to the Tiahuanaco culture in the lake. The past is still being rebuilt in Tiwanaku...

UNESCO

UNESCO's presence in Tiwanaku has proved most beneficial, contributing to the growing tourism. In 2000, it declared Tiwanaku as "Centro Espiritual Y Politico de la Cultura Tiwanaku," meaning a center carrying values of the imperial state of the Tiahuanaco people. With help from the Ministry of Culture and Tourism, Tiwanaku is currently protected by UNESCO specifically as a "sensational tourist experience for foreigners" and a site for present-day archeological studies, as stated on a tablet right outside the ruins. While UNESCO placed Tiwanaku on the map for tourism, its focus on the ruins draws it away from other parts of its culture not touched upon and the town itself. Western organizations such as UNESCO must better represent Tiwanaku instead of labeling it as a "sensational tourist experience," where the people, ceramics, and historical significances are forgotten. Local anthropologists and archeologists in Bolivia are currently making conclusions about the mysteries of the Tiahuanaco ruins and culture, while UNESCO only documents their own research and political circumstances. In their few articles on Tiwanaku, their most important information has to do with President Evo Morales's inauguration in Tiwanaku and their drone topological studies. More must be said about Tiwanaku for the western audience to grasp the culture and amount of local research (with constantly new findings) in this town.

Attractions

Sank'a Uta

(1)

While a bit far from the central town of Tiwanaku, Sank'a Uta is definitely worth the visit for its historical significance and recent discovery. Sank'a Uta is known as a "chullpa", a funeral tower where a noble from the Tiahuanaco and Aymara empires were born. These small castles were built like houses with gigantic rock slabs in a circular shape enough for a body. However, researchers have concluded that Sank'a Uta was not actually a burial site but a punishment dungeon. Historically, leaders of the empires would place subjects in this ruin if they did not pay respects to the gods/deities or the chiefs themselves. Their prisoners would be left there in the cold without food or water, leaving them with a bad name if they made it out alive. Did I also mention that Sank'a Uta means "burning of the soul"? It quite literally did this. Sank'a Uta overall shows the vitality of Tiwanaku's ancient ruins engaging in contemporary research of fascinating background.

Museo Ceramico

(2)

The Ceramic Museum displays a wide variety of pottery and artifacts from the Tiahuanaco Empire. As they are still being discovered today, the collection increases in size. It is possible to see how ceramics are being restored from shattered pieces. As it is near the entrances to both Puma Punku and Akapana Pyramid, it is the perfect place to stop by and appreciate the culture of Tiwanaku. Plus, with admission to both ruin sites, entrance into the museum is FREE.

Akapana, Semisubterraneo, Kalasasaya

(3)

Akapana Pyramid, Semisubterraneo Temple, and Kalasasaya Temple all hold essential significance to the Tiahuanaco Empire. Respectively, these ruins are supposed to resemble heaven, hell, and reality. Akapana Pyramid only has three of its initial seven steps due to conquistador destruction and current excavations. On top, one can get a view of Tiwanaku and the rest of the ruins, similar to how Tiahuanaco priests would view the astronomical signs in the sky. The Semisubterraneo Temple is seen as hell with all the carved out faces on the walls and pit-like structure. The style is unique in how the 25x25m divot is centered with the Dios de Los Baculos. 175 faces surround the center, which depicts the different ethnicities in the Tiahuanaco Empire. Kalasasaya Temple represents reality with a simple plaza look. An arched door outlines the "Monolito Ponce" as well as resembling the first equinox rays that entered perpeniculary when the land was first found. Overall, the architecture is advanced for the empire's period, setting it apart from other past New World civilizations.

Monolito Ponce

(4)

Outside Kalasasaya Temple lies Monolito Ponce. At 3.5 meters tall and 6 tons big, the statue wears a ceremonial mask holding two objects: the representation of religious power and political power. On its right shoulder is a carving of a Christian cross by the Spanish Conquistadors. They actually tried to exorcise all the monoliths with the intention of converting all of the people to Christianity. This cultural difference is worth seeing to really see how tall Mr. Ponce is.

Puerta del Sol

(5)

The Puerta del Sol is one of the most famous ruins here in Tiwanaku, and for a good reason. Made out of one stone slab, the Puerta del Sol places the Dios de Los Baculos as the centerpiece of the gate. There are carvings of human messengers and soldiers helping and protecting the god. Once again, the masonry is impressive, especially with the detailed figures on top of the door. Even though the Puerta del Sol is popular among tourists, other places and activities must not be overlooked.

Puma Punku

(6)

Off to the side, Puma Punku is a whole other area of ruins scattered around. Most of these ruins are H-shaped andesite rocks that historians and scientists had trouble discovering how they were placed. Recently, scientists found out that the stones were actually artificially made rather than brought from the mountains. Technology and knowledge were truly advanced to make Tiahuanaco "cement" from red stone, volcanic leftovers, clay, and organic acids . It is fascinating to see these cement blocks perfectly shaped with 90-degree edges and flawless hole openings.

Town Plaza

(7)

While it might seem obvious to visit the town plaza, many tourists do not do so. A stroll through the plaza would be not only calming but also interesting as one will get to see the influences of the Tiahuanaco Empire on the town. For example, the Catholic Church on the plaza is made out of rock from the Akapana Pyramid. Getting to interact with the locals, grabbing local food to eat, or buying souvenirs to take home are just a few more reasons to see the plaza. It is worth it to stay at a hotel for a couple of days to get the whole experience that Tiwanaku has to offer, starting by being around the Aymara locals and some of the most sacred ruins in the world.
